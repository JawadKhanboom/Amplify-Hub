Stall Tactics

⏱ ~13 min🏆 +60 XP📖 Module 6: Objection Handling

⏳ Soft Stalls Are Not Real Answers

"I need to think about it," "send me some information," and "call me later" are the three most common stalls in sales. None of them are decisions — they're exits from a conversation the prospect doesn't yet feel equipped to finish.

💡 Never ask "what is there to think about?" — it sounds like a challenge. Ask what specifically needs more thought instead.

📋 The Three Most Common Stalls

StallUsually meansBetter response
"I need to think about it."Fear of a mistake, unclear value, or a personal decision rule"Absolutely. So I follow up with something useful instead of just checking in — what part needs more thought?"
"Send me some information."Missing proof, a polite exit, or wanting to share internally"Happy to. What would you like it to help you evaluate?"
"Call me later" / "Now's not a good time."Low urgency, a real timing constraint, or a competing priority"What changes between now and then that makes the timing better?"

Schedule a follow-up only when there's a defined question, action, owner, and date attached to it. "I'll think about it and get back to you" is not a follow-up — it's a stall with no plan behind it.

🔎 Isolate: Is This the Only Thing Stopping Them?

A stall often hides a second concern the prospect hasn't mentioned yet. Before you respond to the stated stall, check whether anything else is sitting underneath it:

  • "Besides that, is there anything else giving you pause?"
  • "If the timing worked, would there be another concern we should talk about?"

Answering one hidden concern while another stays buried just produces a second stall later. Isolate first, then respond to what's actually there — once, not on a loop.

✏️ Practical Exercises

🔍 Exercise 1: Rewrite the challenge

Rewrite "What is there to think about?" as a genuinely curious, non-defensive question.

🔍 Exercise 2: Build a real follow-up

A prospect says "call me next quarter." Write a follow-up commitment that includes a specific question, an owner, a piece of evidence, and a date — not just a vague callback.

🔍 Exercise 3: Isolation drill

A prospect says "I need to think about it," and you suspect there's also a budget concern underneath. Write the exact isolation question you'd ask before responding to either one.

💭 Think of the last stall you accepted at face value. If you'd asked one isolating question, what might have come up?
