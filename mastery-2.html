Building Your Playbook

⏱ ~8 min🏆 +125 XP📖 Module 10: Mastery

📖 More Than a Script

A playbook is not just a script. A script is one piece — the playbook is the complete, repeatable process you use to prepare for calls, run them, review them, and improve them over time.

Top performers don't rely on memory or instinct call to call; they document what works and keep refining it.

The alternative to a playbook isn't flexibility — it's inconsistency. A fixed script sounds robotic and breaks when a prospect says something unexpected. A flexible framework gives you direction while leaving the language open to the moment.

📊 Evidence-Based Updates

Your playbook should evolve from real calls, not guesswork:

  • If prospects consistently get confused at the same point, simplify that section
  • If the same objection keeps surfacing late in calls, address it earlier
  • If you're booking meetings but people aren't showing up, fix your confirmation process
  • If a rep sounds stiff, the fix isn't rewriting their script — it's practising delivery

🧩 The Complete Playbook Structure

  1. Ideal customer profile — who you're actually calling, and who you're not
  2. Pre-call research checklist — what you look up before you dial
  3. Opening framework — how you earn the first few seconds of attention
  4. Discovery-question bank — questions that surface real situations, not surface-level answers
  5. Problem and consequence questions — help the prospect feel the cost of not changing
  6. Qualification criteria — what makes someone worth pursuing
  7. Value explanation — connecting your offer to what they said they need
  8. Objection library — best response to each recurring objection, by category
  9. Meeting-booking language — how you ask for the next step
  10. Follow-up cadence — what happens after the call, and when
  11. Tonality notes — reminders on delivery for key call moments
  12. Call-review scorecard — how you evaluate a recording
  13. Performance metrics — the numbers you actually track
  14. Weekly improvement routine — when and how you update all of the above

📝 Example Playbook Entry

Here's what a single, well-documented playbook entry looks like in practice:

Objection

"Just send me some information."

What's Really Happening

The prospect hasn't felt enough urgency or connected the offer to a specific problem. This is rarely a genuine information request.

Response

"Happy to send something over — so I send you the right thing, can I ask what specifically you'd want it to cover?"

This either surfaces a real concern you can address live, or shows the prospect wasn't actually engaged.

Tonality Note

Curious, not defensive. If this comes out sounding like a challenge, it will backfire.

Update Trigger

If this objection shows up in more than a third of your calls at the same point, move a discovery question earlier to build more urgency before you get here.

✏️ Practical Exercises

🔎 Exercise 1: Draft One Playbook Section

Pick one section of the playbook structure that you don't currently have documented.

  1. Write a first draft of that section based on your real experience
  2. Review your last three calls — does the evidence support what you wrote, or does it need adjusting?
  3. Update the draft based on what the calls actually show

Start with whichever section would make the biggest difference to your next call. Don't try to build the whole playbook at once.

💭 If you lost all your notes tomorrow and had to rebuild your sales process from scratch, which piece would you write first — and which would you struggle most to recreate?
