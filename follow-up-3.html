📝 Follow-Up Scripts That Work

The difference between a follow-up that gets ignored and one that gets a reply usually comes down to specificity and value. Here are six common scenarios with beginner and better versions:

✉️ After no reply to a cold email

Beginner: "Hi [Name], just following up on my last email — did you get a chance to look at it?"

Better: "Hi [Name] — bumping this up in case it got buried. The short version: [one-line value]. Worth a quick look?"

The better version re-states value in one line and makes it easy to say yes or no. The beginner version puts the burden on the prospect with nothing new.

📞 After a good cold call

Beginner: "Hi [Name], following up on our call. Let me know if you have any questions."

Better: "Hi [Name], good talking today. Sending the [resource] I mentioned — take a look and let me know what stands out."

The better version delivers on a promise made during the call and gives a specific next action.

🤔 After "let me think about it"

Beginner: "Hi [Name], have you had a chance to think it over?"

Better: "Hi [Name] — no pressure at all. Out of curiosity, was there a specific part you wanted to think through more?"

The better version opens a conversation about their hesitation instead of just asking for a yes/no update.

📅 After a missed meeting

Beginner: "We had a meeting scheduled and you didn't show. Can we reschedule?"

Better: "Hi [Name] — looks like the times crossed wires. Still worth 15 minutes? Here are two slots."

The better version assumes good intent, keeps it light, and provides an easy path forward.

⏰ After a verbal "not now"

Beginner: "Just checking if now is a better time."

Better: "Hi [Name] — understood on the timing. Would it make sense for me to check back in [timeframe]?"

The better version respects their decision and establishes a concrete next touch point.

👋 Breakup message

Beginner: "I haven't heard back so I'll stop reaching out."

Better: "Hi [Name] — I don't want to keep cluttering your inbox, so I'll leave this here. If [problem] becomes a priority again, I'm happy to pick this back up."

The better version closes gracefully while keeping the door open. It references their problem, not your product.

📄 Proposal Follow-Up

The best proposal follow-up is arranged before the proposal is sent. Most reps send the proposal and then scramble to figure out what happens next. Top performers lock in the next steps before they hit send.

Before you send, clarify:

  • Who will review it?
  • When do they expect to review it?
  • What criteria will they use to evaluate it?
  • Who else needs to be involved in the decision?
  • When will you speak again?

With those answers in hand, your follow-up sequence practically writes itself:

TimingAction
Day of sendingConfirm receipt, restate the agreed review date
+2–3 daysShort check-in tied to the agreed date
+5–6 daysOffer to walk through any open questions live
+10 daysDirect, respectful question about priorities
💡 Pro-Tip: If you don't set the review timeline before sending the proposal, every follow-up after it feels like chasing. Set the frame early.

💬 Handling Common Responses

Not every reply is a "yes" or "no." Most are somewhere in between. Here's how to read what prospects say — and what to do next:

🗃️ CRM and Organization

Follow-up should never depend on memory. If you're trying to remember who you called last week and what they said, you've already lost track of half your pipeline.

Fields to track for every prospect:

  • Prospect name
  • Company
  • Last conversation date
  • Main problem they mentioned
  • Desired result
  • Objections raised
  • Last contact date
  • Next contact date + channel + purpose

Suggested pipeline statuses:

StatusMeaning
NewIdentified but not yet contacted
ContactedFirst outreach sent, no response yet
Conversation startedThey've replied or you've spoken
Proposal sentProposal delivered, awaiting review
Follow-up scheduledActive sequence in progress
NurtureNot ready now, stay in light contact
Not nowTiming issue — revisit later
Closed wonDeal closed successfully
Closed lostDeal lost — log the reason
Do not contactOpted out or explicitly declined
💡 Pro-Tip: A spreadsheet with these fields beats a fancy CRM with empty fields. The tool doesn't matter — the discipline does.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

These are the patterns that kill follow-up effectiveness. If you catch yourself doing any of these, course-correct immediately:

❌ Mistake✅ Correction
Sending the same message repeatedlyAdd a new angle or piece of value each time
Relying on "just checking in"Reference the specific problem or conversation
Following up with no notesLog key details right after every conversation
Contacting too frequentlyWiden gap between later touches
Using fake urgencyOnly use timelines that are actually true
Writing long emailsCut to the relevant point in 2–3 sentences
Ignoring a clear noStop immediately and log the outcome

✅ The 60-Second Follow-Up Checklist

Before you send any follow-up, run through this list. It takes less than a minute and catches the mistakes that cost you replies:

  • What happened in the previous conversation?
  • What problem or goal actually matters to them?
  • Why am I contacting them today, specifically?
  • Am I adding value, or just asking for a status update?
  • Is my message easy to answer in one line?
  • Am I using the right channel for this prospect?
  • Is my timing reasonable given the last touch?
  • What exact next step am I suggesting?
  • Have I scheduled the next action if this doesn't land?
  • Should I continue, nurture, or stop after this?

✏️ Practical Exercise

📝 Exercise 1: Rewrite Three Weak Follow-Ups

Take three follow-up messages you've sent recently (or use the beginner examples from Card 1). Rewrite each one using the AmplifyHub framework:

  1. Reference the specific problem they mentioned
  2. Add a new piece of value (insight, resource, or benchmark)
  3. Make it easy to reply in one line

Compare the before and after. Which version would you reply to?

📅 Exercise 2: Build a Seven-Touch Sequence

Create a complete seven-touch follow-up sequence for a prospect whose proposal has gone quiet. Your sequence must include:

  • At least three different channels (email, phone, LinkedIn, text, etc.)
  • Increasing gaps between later touches
  • A different value angle for each touch
  • A breakup message as the final touch

Map it out day by day with the channel, message angle, and purpose for each touch.

💎 Exercise 3: Write a Value-Based Follow-Up

Pick a real or practice prospect. Write one follow-up message that:

  • References a specific problem they told you about
  • Includes a concrete piece of value (benchmark, case study, resource)
  • Ends with a clear, low-pressure next step

Keep the entire message under five sentences.

🧠 Exercise 4: Continue, Pause, Nurture, or Stop?

For each of these five scenarios, decide whether to continue, pause, nurture, or stop — and explain why:

  1. A prospect said "we went with someone else" but seemed open to staying in touch
  2. A prospect has opened every email but never replied (4 emails sent)
  3. A prospect said "not now, maybe next quarter"
  4. A prospect explicitly asked you to stop emailing them
  5. A prospect replied once enthusiastically but has gone silent for 3 weeks

There's no single right answer for all of these — but there is a wrong one: doing the same thing for every scenario.

🔑 Follow-up works when it gives the prospect a specific, relevant reason to respond. Track every prospect and every next action in a system — never rely on memory. Know when to stop: a clear no means it's time to nurture or move on.
