Discovery Questions · Lesson 1 of 4

Open-Ended Questions — Stop Interrogating, Start Discovering

⏱ ~12 min🏆 +50 XP📖 Module 5: Discovery Questions

🎯 The Discovery Problem

80% of sales calls fail because the rep pitched before the prospect felt understood.

Here's the fix: discovery isn't about collecting answers. It's not an interrogation. It's not a checklist you rush through so you can get to your demo.

Discovery has one job:

Your goal is your prospect reaching their own conclusion that something needs to change.

Read that again. Their conclusion. Not yours. You already know your product solves problems — that's irrelevant. What matters is whether the person across the table arrives at the realization that the status quo is costing them more than they thought.

When that happens, you don't need to pitch. They ask you to.

🗺️ The Discovery Framework

Every great discovery call follows this arc. Miss a stage and the whole thing collapses.

StagePurposeWhat You're After
1. Current SituationUnderstand the landscapeHow things work today — tools, process, team
2. ProblemsSurface frictionWhat's broken, slow, or painful right now
3. ImpactQuantify the costWhat those problems actually cost in time, money, opportunity
4. Desired OutcomePaint the futureWhat "fixed" looks like in the prospect's own words
5. Next StepAdvance the dealA concrete commitment — demo, stakeholder intro, timeline

Most reps nail stages 1-2 and skip straight to pitching. The money is in stage 3 — Impact. That's where the prospect's own math does the selling for you.

💡 Coaching note: If you skip Impact, the prospect will agree they have a problem but never feel enough urgency to buy. They'll say "let me think about it" — which really means "I don't feel the pain enough to act."

🔑 Problem Agreement vs. Need Agreement

These are two completely different jobs, and confusing them kills deals.

Problem AgreementNeed Agreement
"Yes, we have that issue.""Yes, and we need to fix it — now."
Acknowledges a situation existsCommits to taking action on it
Doesn't create urgencyCreates urgency and budget justification
Prospect stays passiveProspect becomes an active buyer

Problem agreement is easy. Everyone has problems. But people live with problems for years.

Need agreement is what moves deals forward. It means the prospect has connected the problem to a cost they're no longer willing to pay. That's your job in discovery.

📋 The 4 Types of Discovery Questions

Every question you ask falls into one of four categories. Master all four and you control the conversation.

TypePurposeExample
SituationMap the current state"Walk me through how your team currently handles inbound leads."
ProblemSurface pain points"Where does that process tend to break down or slow things up?"
ImpactQuantify the cost"When leads slip through the cracks, what does that actually cost you in a typical quarter?"
GoalDefine the desired future"If you could redesign that from scratch, what would the ideal flow look like?"
🔍 More examples for each type

Situation questions:

  • "How many reps are working that territory right now?"
  • "What tools are you using to track pipeline?"
  • "Who owns the decision on this kind of purchase?"

Problem questions:

  • "What's the biggest bottleneck in your current onboarding process?"
  • "If I talked to your reps, what would they say is their #1 frustration?"
  • "Where are you losing the most time each week?"

Impact questions:

  • "If that continues for another year, what's the total cost?"
  • "How does that affect your ability to hit this quarter's number?"
  • "What's the downstream effect on your team's morale?"

Goal questions:

  • "What does 'good enough' look like for this quarter?"
  • "If we fast-forward 6 months and this is solved, what's different?"
  • "What would need to be true for you to feel confident moving forward?"

⚠️ The Impact Trap

Here's what happens when you skip Impact questions:

Prospect says: "Yeah, we definitely have that problem."

You think: "They get it. Time to pitch."

So you launch into your solution. The prospect nods along. The call ends with "sounds interesting, send me some info." And they never respond to your follow-up.

Why? Because acknowledging a problem and feeling compelled to fix it are completely different things. Impact questions are what bridge that gap.

💡 Pro-Tip: A discovery call without impact questions is like a doctor describing symptoms without telling the patient what happens if they don't treat them. No urgency = no action.

✏️ Practical Exercise

🔍 Build Your Question Bank

For your specific product or service, write 3 questions for each type:

  1. 3 Situation questions — mapping the prospect's current world
  2. 3 Problem questions — surfacing where things break
  3. 3 Impact questions — quantifying the cost of inaction
  4. 3 Goal questions — letting them describe their ideal future

Keep them conversational. If a question sounds like it came from a survey, rewrite it until it sounds like something a trusted advisor would ask over coffee.

🔍 The Framework Walkthrough

Take one of your current prospects and map the entire discovery arc:

  1. Current Situation: What do you already know about their world?
  2. Problem: What pain have they hinted at?
  3. Impact: What's the cost you haven't surfaced yet?
  4. Desired Outcome: What would "fixed" look like for them?
  5. Next Step: What's the most logical next commitment?

If you can't fill in stage 3, that's exactly where your next call needs to focus.

💭 Think about your last discovery call. Did the prospect leave feeling understood — or did they leave feeling interrogated? What's the difference?

🎤 Interview Question

"How do you approach discovery on a sales call?"

Example answer: I follow a five-stage framework: Current Situation, Problems, Impact, Desired Outcome, Next Step. The key is spending enough time on Impact — most reps jump from identifying a problem to pitching, but until the prospect quantifies what the problem is costing them, there's no urgency to change. My goal is for the prospect to reach their own conclusion that the status quo isn't working.

💡 Hiring managers want to hear a structured approach. Mentioning "Impact" as the stage most reps skip signals real-world experience, not textbook knowledge.

🔑 Discovery isn't interrogation — it's guiding the prospect to their own conclusion. Master all four question types. Never skip Impact.
