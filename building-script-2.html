Industry-Specific Openers — Make It About Them

⏱ ~12 min🏆 +50 XP📖 Module 3: Building Your Script

🎙️ Your Introduction: 3 Seconds, 3 Pieces

After your opener buys you 10 seconds, the next thing out of your mouth is your introduction. Most reps turn this into a company pitch. That's how you lose the 10 seconds you just earned.

Your introduction has exactly three pieces. No more.

#PieceExample
1Your name"This is Sarah"
2Your company"I'm with Acme Solutions"
3What you do (one sentence)"We help logistics teams cut overtime costs"

That third piece is critical. It's not "We're an enterprise SaaS platform for supply chain optimization." Nobody cares about your category. They care about their problem.

💡 Pro-Tip: Your "what you do" sentence should pass the mom test — could a non-industry person understand it in one hearing? If it requires a glossary, rewrite it.

🔑 The Reason for Call — The Make-or-Break Line

This is where 80% of cold calls die. The reason for call is the single sentence that determines whether your prospect leans in or checks out.

The rule is absolute: your reason for calling must be about the PROSPECT, not about you.

❌ About You✅ About Them
"I'm calling because we just launched a new product...""I'm calling because I noticed your team posted 3 warehouse roles this month..."
"I wanted to introduce our services...""Most VPs in your space are dealing with rising shipping costs — I wanted to see if that's hitting you too."
"We work with companies like yours...""I saw your Dallas expansion — companies scaling that fast usually run into hiring bottlenecks around month 3."
"I'd love to schedule a demo...""I noticed your competitor just switched to automated dispatch — curious if that's putting pressure on your ops team."

See the pattern? The left column talks about the rep. The right column talks about the prospect's world. Same call. Completely different reaction.

🔬 The Observation-Challenge-Question Framework

Building a reason for call that hits is easier when you follow a three-step structure. We call it The OCQ Framework:

StepWhat It DoesExample
ObservationShow you did homework — name something specific"I noticed you're hiring 4 SDRs this quarter..."
ChallengeConnect the observation to a known pain point"...teams scaling that fast usually struggle with ramp time..."
QuestionTurn it into a conversation, not a monologue"...is that something you're running into?"

Observation proves you're not spraying calls at a list. Challenge shows you understand their world. Question hands them the mic.

Three sentences. Under 15 seconds. That's all you need.

🏦 The Reason-for-Call Bank

You don't need a unique reason for every call. You need 5-7 strong observations that apply across your prospect base. Here's a starter bank:

🔍 Common Observations That Start Real Conversations
  • Hiring signal: "I saw you posted [X] roles this month..."
  • Expansion signal: "Congrats on the new [office/location/market]..."
  • Tech change: "I noticed you recently switched to [tool/platform]..."
  • Industry trend: "Most [title]s in [industry] are telling me [specific challenge]..."
  • Competitor move: "Your competitor just [specific action] — curious if that's impacting you..."
  • Pain point pattern: "Teams your size usually hit [specific bottleneck] around this stage..."
  • Regulatory shift: "With the new [regulation/requirement], I'm hearing [specific pain]..."

Pick the ones that match your product. Practice them until they sound natural, not recited.

🔍 Where to Find Observations in Under 60 Seconds
  1. LinkedIn company page — recent posts, headcount changes, "About" section
  2. Job postings — what they're hiring for reveals priorities and pain
  3. Google News — "[company name]" in quotes, filter to last 30 days
  4. Their website — blog posts, press releases, product launches
  5. Prospect's LinkedIn — recent activity, new role, shared content

60 seconds of research buys you 60 seconds of attention. That's the trade-off — and it's wildly in your favor.

🏭 Building Openers for Different Industries

The OCQ framework is universal, but the specific observations change by industry. Here's how the same structure sounds across verticals:

IndustryFull OCQ Opener
SaaS / Tech"I noticed you're hiring 3 AEs — teams scaling revenue that fast usually hit CRM bottleneck around month 2. Is that something your ops team is already seeing?"
Healthcare"I saw your clinic just added a second location — practices expanding right now are telling me patient scheduling across sites is the #1 headache. Has that come up for you?"
Logistics"I noticed your competitor just automated their dispatch — fleet managers in your space are saying the pressure to match is real. Is that on your radar?"
Real Estate"I saw your agency closed 40 deals last quarter — brokerages at that volume usually struggle with follow-up speed on inbound leads. Is that a pain point right now?"
🔍 Exercise: Build Your OCQ for 3 Industries You Sell Into

For each industry you sell into, write one complete OCQ opener:

  1. Observation — What specific signal would you reference?
  2. Challenge — What pain does that signal typically predict?
  3. Question — How would you turn it into an open question?

Write all three. Say them out loud. If any sentence takes more than 5 seconds to deliver, it's too long — cut it.

💭 If your prospect heard your reason for calling and had to guess — would they think "this person did homework" or "this person is reading a list"?

🎤 Interview Question

"How do you research a prospect before calling them?"

Example answer: I spend about 60 seconds per prospect — LinkedIn for recent activity or role changes, their company's job postings for growth signals, and a quick Google News search. I'm looking for one specific observation I can reference in the first 15 seconds. I've found that 60 seconds of prep is the difference between sounding like a stranger and sounding like someone who knows their world.

💡 This question tests process, not knowledge. Managers want to hear a repeatable system, not "I do a lot of research." Specifics beat generalities every time.

🔑 Your reason for calling is about THEM — never you. Observation, Challenge, Question. 60 seconds of research buys 60 seconds of attention.
