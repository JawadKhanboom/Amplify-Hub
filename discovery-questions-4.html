Decision Maker Mapping — Find the Real Buyer

⏱ ~14 min🏆 +50 XP📖 Module 5: Discovery Questions

💀 The 5 Discovery Mistakes That Kill Deals

Before we get into decision maker mapping, let's name the five most common ways reps sabotage their own discovery calls:

MistakeThe Fix
1. Pitching too early — jumping to solutions before the prospect feels the painStay in discovery until the prospect articulates the cost of inaction
2. Asking closed questions — "Do you have this problem?" (Yes/No)Reframe: "Walk me through how that process works today"
3. Talking more than 20% of the timeSet a physical reminder: after you ask, put yourself on mute for 3 seconds
4. Skipping Impact — getting problem agreement without need agreementAlways follow up a problem with "What does that cost you?"
5. Not mapping the decision process — selling to someone who can't buyAsk: "Who else would need to weigh in on something like this?"

Mistake #5 is the silent killer. You can run a perfect discovery call, build massive urgency, and still lose the deal because the person you convinced can't sign the check.

🗺️ Mapping the Decision Process

You need to know three things about every deal:

  • Who decides? — The person who can say yes
  • Who influences? — The people whose opinion the decision maker trusts
  • Who blocks? — The person who can say no even if everyone else says yes

Here are the questions that map this:

QuestionWhat It Reveals
"Who else would need to weigh in on a decision like this?"The full stakeholder map — who has influence
"Walk me through how your team typically evaluates something like this."The buying process — stages, approvals, timelines
"Have you brought in a solution like this before? What happened?"Past buying behavior — successes and failures
"If you were to recommend this internally, what would you need to feel confident doing that?"What your champion needs to sell for you
💡 Pro-Tip: Never ask "Are you the decision maker?" It puts the prospect in an awkward position. Instead, ask about the process — the decision maker's identity will emerge naturally.

🏆 Finding Your Champion

A champion is someone inside the prospect's organization who will sell for you when you're not in the room. They're the most valuable person in any deal.

How to spot one:

  • They feel the pain personally — this problem affects their daily work
  • They have influence — other people listen to their opinion
  • They're willing to spend political capital — they'll go to bat for you

How to create one:

  • Arm them with ammo. Give them the ROI numbers, the competitive data, the business case — everything they need to make the argument internally
  • Coach their pitch. Ask: "When you bring this to [decision maker], what do you think they'll ask?" Then help them prepare answers
  • Make them look good. Position the solution as their idea, their find. Champions sell harder when the win is personal
Without a champion, you're hoping. With a champion, you're planning.

🎬 Discovery in Action

Here's what a real discovery conversation looks like when all the pieces come together. Watch how the rep moves through the framework.

🔍 Full sample conversation: Rep and James (VP of Sales)

Rep: "James, thanks for making time. Before I show you anything, I'd love to understand how things are working on your end. Can you walk me through how your team currently handles inbound leads?"

James: "Sure. Leads come in through the website, marketing qualifies them, and then they get assigned to reps."

Rep: "Got it. And when a lead hits a rep's queue, how quickly are they typically following up?"

James: "Ideally within the hour, but honestly it's probably closer to 24 hours for most reps."

Rep: "Interesting. When there's that gap between the lead coming in and the rep reaching out, what tends to happen?"

James: "We lose a lot of them. By the time we call, they've already talked to a competitor or lost interest."

Rep: "That's frustrating. Do you have a sense of how many leads that affects in a typical month?"

James: "I'd guess about 30-40% of our inbound leads go cold before we get to them."

Rep: "So roughly a third. At your average deal size, what does that represent in lost revenue?"

James: "...it's probably $40-50K a month that's just walking out the door."

Rep: "That's significant. Let me ask — if we fast-forward 6 months and this is solved, what does that look like for your team?"

James: "Every lead gets a call within 15 minutes. Reps aren't cherry-picking. We're actually converting what we're paying marketing to generate."

Rep: "That sounds like a meaningful shift. Who else on your team would need to weigh in on a decision like this?"

James: "My CRO would need to sign off, and our ops team would need to be involved in implementation."

Rep: "Makes sense. If you were to bring this to your CRO, what would they need to see to feel comfortable moving forward?"

Notice: zero pitching. The rep asked 8 questions and James talked himself into urgency. That's discovery done right.

✏️ Practical Exercises

🔍 Build Your 10-Question Discovery Set

Create a customized set of 10 discovery questions for your specific product/market. Include:

  1. 2 Situation questions — mapping their current world
  2. 2 Problem questions — surfacing specific friction
  3. 2 Impact questions — quantifying the cost
  4. 1 Future state question — painting the desired outcome
  5. 1 Budget question — understanding their buying process
  6. 2 Decision maker questions — mapping stakeholders

Memorize these. Not word-for-word — internalize the intent behind each one so they come out naturally in conversation.

🔍 SDR Challenge: Run the Full Framework on 3 Calls

Over the next week, run the complete discovery framework on 3 real calls:

  1. Current Situation → Problems → Impact → Future → Budget → Decision Process
  2. After each call, grade yourself: Did the prospect quantify their pain? Did you identify the decision maker? Did you find a potential champion?
  3. Record the calls (with permission) and listen back for the 80/20 ratio

Success metric: On at least 2 of the 3 calls, the prospect should articulate the cost of their problem in their own words, unprompted by you.

🔍 Champion Identification Worksheet

For each active deal in your pipeline, answer these questions:

  • Who is your champion? (Name and title)
  • Why do they care personally? (What's their specific pain?)
  • Do they have influence? (Will the decision maker listen?)
  • Have you armed them? (Do they have the data to sell internally?)
  • Have you coached their pitch? (Do they know what objections to expect?)

If you can't name a champion for a deal, that deal is at risk. Your next call should focus on building one.

🎤 Interview Question

"Tell me about a deal you won where you weren't initially talking to the decision maker."

Example answer: I was working a deal where my main contact was a Director — great champion, felt the pain deeply, but couldn't sign off on the purchase. Instead of pushing to go around them, I coached them. We worked together on the business case, I helped them anticipate their VP's objections, and I armed them with ROI data in their own language. They presented it internally, and the VP approved it in one meeting. The key was making my champion look like the hero, not trying to replace them.

💡 This answer shows you understand multi-threaded selling and champion development — two things that separate top performers from average reps.

🔑 Map the decision process, never pitch to someone who can't buy, find your champion and arm them to sell when you're not in the room. That's how deals actually close.
