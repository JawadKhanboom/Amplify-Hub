Coaching Others

⏱ ~8 min🏆 +125 XP📖 Module 10: Mastery

🎓 Why Coaching Sharpens Your Own Skills

Teaching someone else forces you to make your own process explicit. When you have to explain WHY a question works — not just that it works — you find gaps in your own understanding.

Coaching others is one of the fastest ways to improve your own calls. The act of breaking down what you do instinctively into clear, teachable steps reveals the parts of your approach you've never examined closely.

💬 Encouragement vs. Feedback

There's a real difference between the two:

  • "Good call, keep it up" feels nice and changes nothing
  • Useful feedback names a specific moment, explains what happened in the prospect's mind, and gives a concrete alternative

Calls need to be recorded and reviewed. Memory is unreliable, and vague recollections produce vague coaching. Without a recording, you're coaching from feelings, not facts.

🎯 The One-Fix Rule

When reviewing a call, resist opening with everything that went wrong.

  1. Start by identifying something the rep did WELL — name it specifically, not generically
  2. Pick the single highest-leverage moment to correct — not the most obvious mistake, the one that would change the outcome if fixed
  3. One or two focused adjustments, practised until automatic, beat a long list every time

It matters WHERE a problem lives. Categories:

CategoryWhat It Means
WordingRight idea, wrong words
StructureWrong order or missing steps
TimingAsking at the wrong moment
TonalityRight question, wrong delivery
ListeningResponding to their script, not the prospect
MindsetEmotional reaction instead of professional response
💡 Coach's Note: Inexperienced coaches often "correct" things that were actually working, because they don't understand why a technique succeeds. Feedback grounded in a real framework protects strengths while improving weak spots.

📋 The Coaching Framework

  1. Select one recorded call to review
  2. Let the rep evaluate their own performance first
  3. Identify one specific strength before anything else
  4. Identify the single highest-impact improvement — not the longest list
  5. Replay the exact moment that matters
  6. Demonstrate a stronger response to that same moment
  7. Roleplay the corrected section together, more than once
  8. Agree on one focus area for the rep's next few calls

📝 Coaching in Action

A rep is reviewing a cold call where the prospect said "just send me some information." On the recording, the rep immediately agreed and moved to close the call.

In review:

  • Strength: The rep stayed calm and didn't sound rattled by the brush-off
  • Highest-impact fix: "Send me information" almost always means the prospect hasn't felt enough urgency yet — it's a stall, not a real request
Better response: "Happy to — what specifically would you want that information to address?"

That question either reveals a real concern or exposes that the prospect wasn't engaged. The rep then roleplays that exact moment two or three times until it comes out naturally.

🏢 Building a Review Culture

  • If your organization has both sales and customer success, review calls together to keep both sides honest about what was promised
  • The goal of any review culture should be shared improvement, not ranking people
  • A team afraid of embarrassment on call review will stop being honest about weak spots
  • Roleplay and repeated practice matter — knowing a better response isn't the same as delivering it under pressure

✏️ Practical Exercises

🔎 Exercise 1: Review One of Your Own Calls

Pull one of your own recent calls (or imagine one in detail). Then work through these steps:

  1. Identify one strength — name the specific moment where you did something well
  2. Identify one highest-impact improvement — what single change would have most affected the outcome?
  3. Write out a stronger response for the weak moment
  4. Say it out loud three times until it sounds natural

Don't skip step 4. Reading a better response silently is not the same as practising delivery.

💭 When was the last time you received specific, actionable feedback on a call — not just "good job" or "try harder"? What was the single most useful correction anyone ever gave you?
