Discovery and Objection Handling

⏱ ~10 min🏆 +100 XP📖 Module 9: Live Practice

🔍 Discovery During a Live Call

Once you've earned permission, move from opening into discovery. Your job now is to understand their world before you talk about yours. Areas to explore:

  • Current process — how they handle it today
  • Current problem — where the pain lives
  • Impact of that problem — what it costs in time, money, or morale
  • Desired result — what "better" looks like to them
  • What they've already tried — past solutions and why they fell short
  • How much of a priority this is — where it sits on their list
  • Timing — when they need it solved

Natural discovery questions:

  • "How are you currently handling that?"
  • "What's the biggest challenge with the current process?"
  • "How is that affecting the team?"
  • "What would you want to improve, if anything?"
  • "Is this something you're actively trying to solve right now?"
💡 Pro-Tip: Discovery isn't an interrogation. Space your questions with natural reactions — "That makes sense," "I hear that a lot" — so the conversation flows instead of feeling like a checklist.

💡 Exploring Vague Answers

Prospects often give broad, noncommittal answers. That's normal — they're testing whether you'll accept the surface or dig deeper. Here's how to explore a vague response:

Prospect
"We need more pipeline."
Follow-up
"Which part is weakest right now — starting conversations, booking meetings, or converting the meetings you do get?"
Follow-up
"How far below target is the team currently?"
Follow-up
"What have you already tried to fix it?"
💡 A vague answer isn't a dead end — it's an invitation to ask one more specific question.

👂 Listening and Adapting

A skilled seller follows the conversation, not just the script. Listen for:

  • Problems — what's broken or frustrating
  • Goals — where they want to be
  • Numbers — revenue, headcount, growth rate
  • Emotion — frustration, excitement, hesitation
  • Uncertainty — "I'm not sure," "maybe," "it depends"
  • Decision-makers — who else is involved
  • Timing — deadlines, quarters, fiscal years
  • Contradictions — saying one thing but describing another
  • Specific words they use — their vocabulary for the problem
💡 Reuse the prospect's own language in your next question — it proves you were actually listening.

⚠️ Signs You're Not Listening

  • Asking a question they already answered
  • Jumping straight to the pitch
  • Repeating the script regardless of response
  • Interrupting
  • Ignoring important details they mentioned
  • Giving long answers when short ones would do

If you catch yourself doing any of these, pause, take a breath, and refocus on what they just said.

🔄 Reframing and Clarifying

When a prospect gives vague pushback — "I need to think about it," "send me some info" — don't respond immediately. Clarify first:

  1. Acknowledge what they said
  2. Show the concern is reasonable
  3. Ask a clarifying question
  4. Respond only after you understand

Reframing examples:

Prospect saysClarifying response
"I need to think about it.""Totally fair. What's the main thing you'd want to think through?"
"I don't have time.""Understood. Is it the timing this week, or is this not a priority right now?"
"We already have a provider.""Good to know. What's working well with them, and what would make you consider a change?"
"I need to speak with my spouse.""Of course. What do you think would matter most to them about this?"
"I'm not sure this would work.""What makes you unsure — is it the approach, the fit, or something else?"
💡 Coach's Note: Reframing is for clarifying vague concerns — not for dodging direct questions. If a prospect asks "How much does this cost?", answer it honestly. Deflecting real questions with more questions erodes trust.

⚖️ Ethical Objection Handling

The goal is to understand the concern, not to defeat the prospect. Use this sequence:

  1. Acknowledge — show you heard them
  2. Clarify — make sure you understand the real concern
  3. Isolate — is this the only concern?
  4. Explore — why does this matter to them?
  5. Respond — address what you now understand
  6. Confirm — does that address it?

This sequence keeps the conversation respectful and productive. The prospect should feel heard, not cornered.

🚫 Never Use These Tactics

These approaches are manipulative, damaging to trust, and have no place in ethical sales:

  • Shaming a prospect for their situation or financial position
  • Applying pressure through a prospect's spouse or family
  • Manufacturing fear or urgency that isn't real
  • Arguing to "win"
  • Ignoring stated budget or financial limits
  • Undermining a spouse's role in a shared decision
  • Refusing to accept a legitimate, repeated no
  • Making promises about results you can't guarantee

If a prospect says no and means it, the ethical response is to accept it and move on.

💭 Think about the last objection you heard. Did you truly understand the concern before responding — or did you react to the surface words?
