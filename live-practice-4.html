Call Review and Roleplay

⏱ ~10 min🏆 +100 XP📖 Module 9: Live Practice

🎯 Transitioning to the Next Step

Discovery without a clear next step is just a nice conversation. Once you've uncovered a real problem, move from discovery into a meeting:

  1. Summarize what the prospect said
  2. Connect the problem to the next conversation
  3. Explain what the meeting will provide
  4. Ask for a specific time

Transition examples:

"From what you've shared, your reps are creating activity but not enough qualified pipeline. It could help to review your messaging and call process together — would Tuesday afternoon or Wednesday morning work better?"
"Sounds like the team's growing fast and onboarding hasn't caught up yet. Worth 20 minutes to see where the gaps are?"
💡 Coach's Note: Confirm the meeting lands on their calendar before you hang up. Ask them to check while you're still on the phone.

📊 Learning From Failed Calls

Every failed call is a dataset. The goal isn't to avoid failure — it's to extract the lesson before moving on.

What happenedWhy it failedThe lesson
Prospect kept asking "who is this?" and hung upRushing past identification when the line is unclearConfirm you can be heard clearly before diving into your opener
Used internal team jargon prospect didn't recognizeGuessing at insider language confuses more than it impressesLead with plain-language problems, not internal terminology
Gave a long tactical explanation to a CROMismatch — senior stakeholders think in outcomes, not mechanicsMatch the altitude of conversation to the seniority of the person
Prospect said business was going well, no urgencyNot every prospect has an active, felt problemAccept the disqualification gracefully and move on

🎭 Roleplay Scenarios

Practise these scenarios with a partner, your AI coach, or by recording yourself. Each one targets a different real-world challenge.

🎯 Scenario 1: Busy Leader

Who: VP of Sales, back-to-back meetings all day.

Opening: "I've got about 90 seconds — go."

Main problem: Pipeline quality is inconsistent.

Hidden concern: New tool = more admin work for the team.

Your objective: Earn 20 more seconds of attention and book a discovery call.

🔬 Scenario 2: Skeptical Owner

Who: Small home-services business owner, skeptical of sales calls.

Opening: "We're doing fine, not looking for anything."

Hidden concern: Burned by an overpromising vendor before.

Your objective: Find out if there's a real problem beneath the dismissal.

📩 Scenario 3: Warm Inbound

Who: Marketing manager who downloaded a guide two days ago.

Opening: "Oh right, yeah, I did grab that."

Hidden concern: Early in the research process, doesn't want pressure.

Your objective: Understand what prompted the download and qualify without pushing.

👥 Scenario 4: Partner Concern

Who: Small business owner deciding jointly with their spouse.

Opening: "I'd want to run this by my spouse first."

Hidden concern: None — this is legitimate.

Your objective: Ask what would matter most to their partner and offer to include them in the next conversation.

📅 The Seven-Day Practice Plan

Consistent practice beats occasional cramming. Follow this plan for one week to build real muscle memory:

DayFocus
Day 1Practise openings
Day 2Practise discovery questions
Day 3Practise objection handling
Day 4Make live calls; record where permitted
Day 5Review calls and select one weakness
Day 6Roleplay the weak section
Day 7Repeat the improved version live
💡 Pro-Tip: After Day 7, start the cycle again with a new weakness. Improvement compounds — one fix per week is 52 fixes per year.

⚠️ Common Mistakes

❌ Mistake✅ Correction
Over-preparing and sounding roboticPrepare key points, not word-for-word scripts
Talking too quicklySlow down deliberately in the opening
Pitching features immediatelyAsk about their problem first
Asking questions without listeningPause and process the answer
Arguing with objectionsAcknowledge and clarify first
Avoiding direct questionsAnswer honest questions honestly
Failing to ask for the meetingAlways close discovery with a specific ask
Trying to correct ten skills at oncePick one weakness per practice cycle

✏️ Practical Exercises

🎤 Exercise 1: Record Three Openings

Pick three different prospect types you commonly call. Record a permission-based opening for each one — out loud, on your phone or laptop. Listen back and ask:

  • Did I sound natural or scripted?
  • Would this person give me 30 more seconds?
  • Did I reference something specific to them?
🔄 Exercise 2: Turn Pitches Into Problems

Take five of your current product pitches and rewrite each one as a problem-based statement — lead with what the prospect is struggling with, not what your product does.

Example:

Before: "We offer AI-powered call coaching for sales teams."

After: "A lot of sales leaders tell us their new reps take 4+ months to ramp — and half leave before they hit quota. We help cut that ramp time in half."

📋 Exercise 3: Complete a Scorecard

Pull up one of your own recent calls (recorded or from memory) and fill out the Call-Review Scorecard above. Be honest — the score itself doesn't matter. What matters is:

  • Which category scored lowest?
  • What one change would raise it by 3+ points next time?
🎭 Exercise 4: Full Roleplay

Complete one full roleplay scenario from the cards above — ideally Scenario 1 (Busy Leader). Use the AI Coach, a colleague, or record yourself playing both sides. Then score it with the scorecard.

Focus on: opening under pressure, discovery in under 2 minutes, and transitioning to a specific meeting time.

🔑 The gap between a beginner and a strong closer is almost always repetition with real feedback, not raw talent. Treat your early recordings as data, not evidence you aren't cut out for sales.
