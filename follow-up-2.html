Multi-Channel Follow-Up

⏱ ~10 min🏆 +50 XP📖 Module 8: Follow-up

⏰ Timing and Frequency

There is no single correct number of follow-ups. The right cadence depends on industry, deal size, cycle length, channel, and how the prospect responded to your last touch.

That said, the reps who get the most replies share a common pattern:

The Pattern That Works:
• Spread contact across multiple days — never compress into 24 hours
• Use more than one channel — single-channel reliance lowers response rates
• Widen gaps between later touches — early touches can be closer together, later ones further apart
• Batch touches on contact days — pairing email with a call on the same day increases contact rates

Think of your sequence as a rhythm, not a script. Early = closer together. Late = more space. And every touch should use a different angle or channel than the one before it.

📱 Channel-Specific Guidance

Each channel has its own rules. Treating them all the same is a fast way to get ignored — or blocked.

✉️ Email

Keep messages short, relevant, and easy to reply to in one line. Use personalized openers that reference their situation, not generic intros.

Stay in the same thread. Bumping a previous email is more effective than starting a new one — it gives context without making them dig.

Avoid "just checking in" as your entire message. Every email should contain at least one new angle or piece of value.

📞 Phone

Reference the previous conversation immediately. Open with context: "Last time we spoke you mentioned X — I wanted to follow up on that."

Don't re-pitch from scratch. The prospect already heard your intro. Move the conversation forward, not sideways.

If they don't answer, leave a voicemail and pair it with a follow-up email on the same day.

💬 Voicemail

No full pitch. A voicemail is a pointer, not a presentation.

Structure: one sentence of relevance ("You mentioned slow lead response"), one sentence of context ("I sent over a benchmark on that"), and a pointer to email ("Check your inbox — it's short").

Keep it under 30 seconds. Anything longer gets deleted before it finishes.

👥 LinkedIn

LinkedIn is a strong secondary channel. Use it to reinforce your email or phone touches, not to repeat them.

Don't repost your email verbatim as a LinkedIn message. Keep it conversational and brief.

Connection requests work fine without a note — a blank request often has a higher acceptance rate than a sales pitch in the note field.

📱 Text / WhatsApp

Only use text or WhatsApp where the prospect has given you that number and the context is appropriate.

Keep messages short and low-pressure. No hard sell. A quick "Hey [Name], sent you something worth a look — check your email when you get a sec" works better than a pitch.

This channel works best for prospects you've already spoken with at least once.

💬 Coach's Note: "Don't hit every channel on the same day out of frustration. Spreading touches across the week keeps follow-up professional."

💎 Value-Based Follow-Up

Every follow-up should give the prospect something. If your message only asks for a status update, you're withdrawing from the relationship without depositing anything.

Here are ten things you can bring to a follow-up:

  • A relevant insight or data point
  • A short, similar case study
  • A useful resource or template
  • An industry benchmark
  • An observation from their business
  • A direct answer to their question
  • A new idea related to their goal
  • A relevant industry update
  • A short testimonial from a similar customer
  • A simple, specific recommendation

See the difference:

❌ Weak✅ Better
"Just checking whether you had a chance to see my last email.""You mentioned slow lead response was costing booked demos — I found a benchmark on how response time affects conversion. Want me to send it over?"

The weak version asks for something. The better version gives something — and ties it directly to the prospect's stated problem.

🔗 Reconnecting to the Original Problem

When a prospect goes quiet, the strongest move is to anchor back to the specific problem they told you about — not your product, not your features, not your timeline.

"We spoke recently about [problem], which was affecting [impact] and getting in the way of [desired result]. Has anything changed since then?"

This works because it proves you listened. You're not pitching — you're checking whether the problem still exists. That's a fundamentally different posture.

Low-pressure alternatives:

  • "Is this still something you're trying to improve?"
  • "Has this moved lower on the priority list?"
  • "Did you end up finding another way to solve it?"
  • "Would it make more sense to revisit this at a later point?"

Each of these gives the prospect an easy out. Paradoxically, giving people permission to say no makes them more likely to say yes.

💭 Think about a prospect you lost contact with. What specific problem did they mention — and what new value could you bring to reopen that conversation?
