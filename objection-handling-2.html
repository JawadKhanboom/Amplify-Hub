Price Objections

⏱ ~14 min🏆 +60 XP📖 Module 6: Objection Handling

💰 "Too Expensive" Is Five Different Objections

When a prospect says "it's too expensive," most reps hear one thing: no money. In reality, that sentence hides at least five distinct problems, and each one needs a completely different response.

PossibilityWhat's really going onClarifying question
Weak perceived valueThe outcome doesn't yet feel worth the number"Which outcome doesn't yet justify the cost?"
Genuine lack of fundsIt's outside what's safely available right now"Is this simply outside what's available right now?"
Cash-flow timingThe total is fine, the timing of payment isn't"Would a later start solve it, or is the total still off?"
Fear of losing moneyWorried about risk, not the number itself"What evidence or protection would help you assess the risk?"
Competing priorityThe budget is being weighed against something else"What is this being weighed against?"
💡 A genuine inability to pay is a condition to respect, not a challenge to overcome. Never push debt, hardship payments, or money needed for essentials.

🅲 Clarify Before You Respond (C of PACER)

Never answer a price objection before you know which of the five it is. Clarifying isn't stalling — it's the only way to avoid solving the wrong problem.

❌ Reflex response✅ Clarify first
Immediately offering a discount"Is the concern the amount, the timing, or whether the return justifies it?"
Launching into ROI math unprompted"Which outcome doesn't yet justify the cost?"
Assuming they have no money"Is this outside what's safely available, or more of a timing question?"

Once you know which version of "expensive" you're dealing with, the right move is usually obvious — and it's never the same move twice.

📊 Compare Cost to Value, Never Discount First

After clarifying, compare cost and expected value using conservative assumptions the prospect already agreed to earlier in the call. Offer only appropriate payment structures — a payment plan solves a cash-flow issue, not a value issue.

  • Value gap: revisit the specific outcome they said mattered most, and connect the cost to that outcome directly.
  • Cash-flow gap: offer a realistic payment structure or start date — never a fake discount to mask a value problem.
  • Real budget limit: respect it. Don't manufacture financing that puts the prospect at risk.
💡 A discount offered before clarification tells the prospect the first number was never real. Clarify first, and only then decide whether a structural change — not a lower price — solves the actual issue.

✏️ Practical Exercises

🔍 Exercise 1: Diagnose the five

For each scenario, name which of the five price-objection types is most likely, and write the clarifying question you'd ask: (1) "That's a lot for what it is." (2) "We just don't have that in the budget this quarter." (3) "I like it, but I'm nervous about committing that much." (4) "We're also considering a new hire with that money."

🔍 Exercise 2: Rewrite the discount reflex

Rewrite this response to clarify first instead of discounting: Prospect: "It's too expensive." Rep: "I can knock 15% off if you sign today."

🔍 Exercise 3: Value vs. cash-flow role-play

Practice out loud: a prospect says the total is fine but they can't pay it all this month. Respond with a cash-flow structure, not a value pitch. Then reverse it — the prospect has the cash but doubts the value — and respond with evidence, not a payment plan.

💭 Next time you hear "too expensive," which of the five will you assume it is — and how will you find out you're wrong before you respond?
