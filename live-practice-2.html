The First 30 Seconds

⏱ ~10 min🏆 +100 XP📖 Module 9: Live Practice

🎯 The Opening Structure

Most cold calls are won or lost in the opening. The prospect decides whether to keep listening or start looking for an exit — and that decision happens before you finish your second sentence.

Use this five-part structure to earn the right to continue:

  1. Identify yourself — first name, company if relevant
  2. Mention relevant context — why you're calling THIS person
  3. Ask permission to continue — give them a moment to opt in
  4. Explain the reason for the call — one sentence
  5. Introduce a problem or observation — not a pitch

Notice what's not in the structure: a product demo, a feature list, or a 90-second monologue about your company. The opening earns attention. The conversation earns the meeting.

💬 Opening Examples

Each scenario calls for a slightly different opener. Tap to expand:

❄ Completely cold prospect

"Hi [Name], it's [You] with [Company]. I know I'm catching you out of nowhere — got 20 seconds for me to share why I called?"

Why it works: You acknowledge the interruption honestly. You ask for a tiny commitment (20 seconds) instead of demanding open-ended time. It feels human, not scripted.

📈 Prospect tied to a company event

"Hi [Name], saw you're hiring a wave of account executives right now — that's actually why I called. Got a minute?"

Why it works: The trigger (hiring AEs) signals you did your homework. The prospect immediately understands this isn't a random dial.

🤝 Warm lead (referral)

"Hi [Name], [Referrer] suggested I reach out — mentioned you'd been looking at ways to [goal]. Is now an okay time?"

Why it works: The referrer's name creates instant trust. Mentioning the goal shows you aren't cold — you're continuing a conversation someone else started.

📄 Downloaded a resource

"Hi [Name], saw you grabbed our guide on [topic] — wanted to see what prompted the interest, if you've got a minute."

Why it works: You reference a specific action they took. The question ("what prompted the interest") invites them to talk about their problem, not listen to yours.

⚖️ Weak vs. Strong Openings

Side by side, the difference is obvious. In practice, most reps default to the left column without realising it.

❌ Weak (product-heavy)✅ Strong (problem-based)
"Hi, we offer a full suite of solutions for sales teams including automation, reporting, and coaching tools — do you have a few minutes?" "Hi [Name], most sales leaders I talk to are seeing reps spend more time on admin work than actual selling — is that something you're running into too?"

The weak opening talks about you. The strong opening talks about them. That single shift changes whether the next sound is a click or a conversation.

⚠ Important: Never argue with a clear refusal. "Take me off your list" and a firm "not interested" after you've tried once both mean stop. Pushing past a genuine no doesn't win business — it burns the relationship and your reputation.

🎤 Tonality and Delivery

The same words land differently depending on delivery. Five principles to internalise:

  • Pace your speech — don't rush through the opener
  • Use pauses instead of filler words ("um," "uh," "like")
  • Speak clearly at natural volume
  • Smile naturally — it changes tone even when unseen
  • Stay composed after rejection — frustration leaks into the next call

Same Line, Three Deliveries:

Nervous
"Um, hi, is, uh, is this a bad time? I can call back if..."
Aggressive
"You need to hear this — it'll change how you sell, period."
Calm and confident
"Hi [Name], got 20 seconds? I think this is relevant to what you're working on."
🎤 Recording Exercise: Record yourself delivering your opening in all three tones above. Play it back. Most beginners are surprised by how much faster or flatter they sound compared to how it feels in the moment.
💭 Record your opening line right now — even on your phone. Play it back. Does it sound like a confident professional, or does it need work?
