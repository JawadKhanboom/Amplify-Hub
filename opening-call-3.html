📋 What to Know Before You Dial

Most reps don't realize this: the call is won or lost before you pick up the phone. The five questions below are your pre-dial checklist. If you cannot answer all five, you are not ready to call.

#QuestionWhy It Matters
1Who am I calling and what is their role?You need to match your language to their world
2What does their company do?Shows basic respect — prospects detect zero-prep instantly
3What problem might they have that I can solve?This IS your reason for calling — without it, you're just interrupting
4What trigger or signal made me pick THIS person?Turns a cold call into a warm-ish call with one sentence
5What is my one-sentence reason for the call?If you can't say it in one sentence, the prospect won't hear it at all
💡 Pro-Tip: Five minutes of prep separates you from 90% of callers. Most reps skip it. That is not a reason to skip it — it is the reason prep works.

🔍 Prospect Prep Checklist

🔍 Full pre-call research checklist

LinkedIn (60 seconds):

  • Current title, time in role, previous roles
  • Recent posts or activity — anything you can reference
  • Mutual connections or shared groups

Company (60 seconds):

  • What they sell, who they sell to
  • Recent news: funding, product launch, expansion, layoffs
  • Job postings — hiring signals reveal priorities

Your CRM (30 seconds):

  • Any prior touchpoints? Emails opened? Previous calls?
  • Notes from other reps who have contacted this account

Total time: under 3 minutes. This is not optional. This is the minimum.

🎙️ Delivery: The 85% That Actually Matters

Remember the 85/15 rule from Module 1. Here is what that looks like in practice — the three delivery pillars that separate booked meetings from busy signals.

❌ What Bad Delivery Sounds Like✅ What Good Delivery Sounds Like
Fast, rushed, trying to squeeze words in before they hang upCalm, measured pace — like talking to a colleague, not racing a timer
High-pitched, nervous energy — "sales voice"Lower register, confident without being aggressive
Monotone script readingNatural emphasis on key words, varied cadence
No pauses — filling every gap with more wordsStrategic pauses after key questions — silence creates space for thinking

The three pillars:

  • Confidence — you believe you belong in this conversation. Not arrogant. Certain.
  • Calm Energy — your pace says "I have something worth hearing" not "please don't hang up."
  • Natural Delivery — you sound like yourself having a conversation, not a performer reciting lines.

🔄 The Opening Transition Structure

Once your opener lands and the prospect gives you a few more seconds, you need to transition smoothly into the reason for the call. Here is the structure:

PhaseDurationWhat Happens
1. Opening5-10 secIdentity + Context + Relevance (the hook)
2. Reason For Call10-15 secOne sentence connecting their world to a problem you solve
3. Problem Question5-10 secA sharp question that "pokes the bear" — surfaces a pain point

The whole thing takes 20-35 seconds. After the problem question, you stop talking. The prospect's response tells you whether this is a real conversation or a polite exit.

🐻 The "Poke the Bear" Question

This is the single most important question in the opening. It takes the general reason for your call and turns it into a specific, uncomfortable truth the prospect has to confront.

💡 Example: "Most sales leaders I talk to say their biggest headache right now isn't hiring — it's that new reps take 6+ months to ramp. Is that something your team is dealing with?"

Why "poke the bear" works:

  • It names a real problem — not your product, not your solution, the problem itself
  • It creates urgency — by quantifying the pain ("6+ months"), you make it concrete
  • It invites honesty — "is that something you're dealing with?" is a genuine question, not a setup
🔍 More "Poke the Bear" examples by industry

SaaS to VP Sales: "Most VPs I talk to say their pipeline looks healthy on paper, but when they dig in, 40% of it hasn't been touched in 30 days. Is that showing up for you?"

Recruiting to HR Director: "I keep hearing that time-to-fill has gone from 45 to 90 days in the last year for mid-market companies. Is your team seeing the same thing?"

MarTech to CMO: "A lot of marketing leaders I talk to are spending more on paid channels but seeing diminishing returns on CAC. Is that a conversation happening internally?"

Cybersecurity to CTO: "Most CTOs I work with say they feel confident in their perimeter security — until I ask about lateral movement detection. How are you handling that today?"

🔗 The Discovery Transition

If the prospect engages with your "poke the bear" question, you have earned the right to go deeper. The transition sounds like this:

💡 Example: "Interesting — a few other [similar role] have told me the same thing. Would it make sense to spend 15 minutes this week walking through how we've helped [similar company] cut that from 6 months to 8 weeks? If it's not a fit, worst case you get a framework you can use internally."

Notice the structure: validate their answer → social proof → low-commitment ask → safety net.

The safety net ("worst case you get a framework") removes the prospect's fear that they're committing to a sales pitch. You are offering value even if they never buy.

💭 Record yourself delivering your opener three times. On the third take, are you still reading — or are you talking? That is the difference between 15% and 85%.

🎤 Interview Question

"How do you prepare for a cold call? Walk me through your process."

Example answer: I spend about 2-3 minutes per prospect. I check LinkedIn for their title, time in role, and any recent posts. I look at the company for recent news — funding, hiring, product launches. Then I write one sentence: the specific reason I'm calling this person. If I can't articulate that in one sentence, I'm not ready to dial.

💡 Hiring managers want to hear a repeatable process, not "I wing it." Even if your process is simple, the fact that you have one puts you ahead of most candidates.

🔑 Prep before you dial. Deliver with confidence, calm energy, and natural tone. Open → Reason → Poke the Bear. Then stop talking and listen.
